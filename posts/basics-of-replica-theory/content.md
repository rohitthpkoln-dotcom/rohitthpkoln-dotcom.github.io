## Overview

The replica method is a way of converting an average over a logarithm, or an average over a normalized probability distribution, into the study of several coupled copies of the same statistical system. While originally developed to study disordered systems in statistical mechanics, replica theory has become an essential framework for analyzing the high-dimensional landscapes of neural networks, black hole entropy, and machine learning. It is also widely applied in information theory to determine the fundamental limits of error-correcting codes.

Here we will learn two closely related but physically distinct limits of the replica trick. In the usual theory of quenched disorder, one introduces \(n\) replicas and eventually takes \(n \to 0\). In measurement or Bayesian conditioning problems, the measurement outcomes play a role analogous to a *conditional* disorder. In this case the useful analytic continuation is instead an \(N \to 1\) replica limit. In this note, \(n\) and \(N\) are both replica numbers, not to be confused with numbers of degrees of freedom.

> In the disorder problem, replicas are introduced to average over a quenched random Hamiltonian.  In the measurement problem, replicas are introduced to average over measurement outcomes shared by several posterior samples.  This difference changes the replica limit: \(n\to0\) for quenched disorder, but \(N\to1\) for Bayesian or measurement-conditioned ensembles.

## Disorder problem \(n \to 0\)

We begin with the standard example of an Ising spin glass.  The microscopic degrees of freedom are Ising spins \(\sigma_i=\pm1\), and the Hamiltonian is
\[
H = -\sum_{\langle ij\rangle} J_{ij}\,\sigma_i \sigma_j .
\]
Here the couplings \(J_{ij}\) are random variables.  They are called *quenched* because they are fixed for a given sample: the spins thermally fluctuate while the disorder realization does not.  For a fixed disorder realization \(J\), the partition function is \(Z[J]\), and the free energy is
\[
F[J]=-T\ln Z[J].
\]
The physical disorder-averaged free energy is therefore
\[
\overline{F} = -T\,\overline{\ln Z[J]} .
\]
The difficulty is the logarithm.  It is not generally easy to average \(\ln Z[J]\) directly.  The replica trick replaces the logarithm by the identity
\[
\ln Z = \lim_{n\to 0}\frac{Z^n - 1}{n} .
\]
For positive integer \(n\), \(Z^n\) has a concrete interpretation: it is the partition function of \(n\) independent copies of the same system. The important point is that these copies are independent thermally, but they all see the *same disorder realization*.

\[\overline{Z^n} \text{ is interpreted as } n \text{ independent copies (replicas)
of the system with identical disorder realization.}
\]
After computing \(\overline{Z^n}\) for integer \(n\), one analytically continues the result in \(n\) and finally sends \(n\to0\).

For the replicated system we write the spin in replica \(a\) as \(\sigma_i^{(a)}\), with \(a=1,\dots,n\).  The disorder-averaged replicated partition function is

\[
\begin{aligned}
\overline{Z^n}
&=
\sum_{\{\sigma^a\}}
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left\langle
\exp\!\left(
\beta J_{ij}\sum_{a=1}^n \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
\right)
\right\rangle_J .
\end{aligned}
\]

Before disorder averaging, the replicas are independent.  After disorder averaging, the same random coupling \(J_{ij}\) appears in every replica, so the average generates effective interactions between replicas.

For Gaussian disorder, for each bond define
\[
S_{ij}\equiv \sum_{a=1}^n \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}.
\]
Assume \(J_{ij}\) is Gaussian with mean \(J_0\) and variance \(\Delta^2\).  Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
\left\langle e^{\beta J_{ij}S_{ij}}\right\rangle_J
&=
\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{dJ}{\sqrt{2\pi\Delta^2}}\,
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{(J-J_0)^2}{2\Delta^2}
\right)
\exp\!\left(
\beta J \sum_a \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
\right)
\\[4pt]
&\propto
\exp\!\left(
\beta J_0 \sum_a \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+
\frac{\beta^2\Delta^2}{2}
\left(\sum_a \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\right)^2
\right) .
\end{aligned}
\]

The first term is simply the interaction generated by the mean coupling \(J_0\).  The second term is more important: it is produced by the fluctuations of the disorder and is quadratic in the sum over replicas.

The replica-replica interaction comes from expanding

\[
\begin{aligned}
\left(\sum_a \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\right)^2
&=
\sum_a\sum_b
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
\\
&=
n + 2\sum_{a<b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(b)} .
\end{aligned}
\]

The diagonal terms \(a=b\) give a constant \(n\), since \((\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)})^2=1\).  The off-diagonal terms \(a\neq b\) generate a genuine coupling between different replicas.

Therefore, up to an additive constant, the disorder-averaged replicated model is

\[
\begin{aligned}
\overline{Z^n}
&=
\sum_{\{\sigma^a\}}
\exp\!\Bigg[
K_1 \sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sum_a \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+
K_2 \sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sum_{a<b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
\Bigg]
\end{aligned}
\]

with
\[
K_1 = \beta J_0,
\qquad
K_2 = \beta^2\Delta^2 .
\]
Thus the original disordered system has been replaced by a translationally invariant interacting theory of replicas.  The price is that the final answer is obtained by analytic continuation to \(n\to0\) if we want to calculate the disorder averaged free energy.

### Correlation functions

The same idea also explains how to compute disorder-averaged observables.  For a fixed realization \(J\),
\[
\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j\rangle_J
=
\frac{1}{Z[J]}
\sum_{\{\sigma\}}
\sigma_i \sigma_j\, e^{-\beta H[\sigma,J]} .
\]
Averaging over disorder gives
\[
\overline{\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j\rangle}
=
\overline{
\left(
\frac{1}{Z[J]}
\sum_{\{\sigma\}}
\sigma_i \sigma_j\, e^{-\beta H[\sigma,J]}
\right)
} .
\]
To express the factor \(1/Z[J]\) using replicas, write

\[
\begin{aligned}
\overline{\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j\rangle}
&=
\overline{
\left(
\frac{
\sum_{\{\sigma\}}
\sigma_i \sigma_j\, e^{-\beta H[\sigma,J]}\, Z^{\,n-1}[J]
}{
Z^n[J]
}
\right)
} .
\end{aligned}
\]

Using the replica trick \(n\to0\), so that \(Z^n[J]\to 1\) for each disorder realization \(J\),

\[
\begin{aligned}
\overline{\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j\rangle}
&=
\lim_{n\to 0}
\overline{
\left(
\sum_{\{\sigma\}}
\sigma_i \sigma_j\, e^{-\beta H[\sigma,J]}\, Z^{\,n-1}[J]
\right)
} .
\end{aligned}
\]

Now

\[
\begin{aligned}
Z^{\,n-1}[J]
&=
\prod_{a=2}^{n} Z[J]
=
\prod_{a=2}^{n}
\sum_{\{\sigma^a\}} e^{-\beta H[\sigma^a,J]} .
\end{aligned}
\]

Label the original sum \(\{\sigma\}\) as \(\{\sigma^{(1)}\}\).  Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
\overline{\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j\rangle}
&=
\lim_{n\to 0}
\overline{
\sum_{\{\sigma^a\}_{a=1}^{n}}
\sigma_i^{(1)}\sigma_j^{(1)}
\exp\!\left(
-\beta \sum_{a=1}^{n} H[\sigma^a,J]
\right)
} .
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus the physical disorder-averaged correlation function is represented as a correlation function in the replicated theory:
\[
\overline{\langle \sigma_i \sigma_j\rangle}
=
\lim_{n\to 0}
\left\langle
\sigma_i^{(1)}\sigma_j^{(1)}
\right\rangle_{\mathrm{rep}} .
\]
The choice of replica \(1\) is arbitrary because the replicated theory is symmetric under permutation of replicas.

A particularly important spin-glass observable is the Edwards--Anderson order parameter. It measures whether local magnetization is frozen in a typical state, even if the disorder-averaged magnetization vanishes. In the replica language,
\[
q_{\mathrm{EA}}
=
\overline{\langle \sigma_i\rangle^2}
=
\lim_{n\to 0}
\left\langle
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}
\right\rangle_{\mathrm{rep}},
\qquad a\neq b.
\]
The product of two distinct replicas appears because the square \(\langle\sigma_i\rangle^2\) means two independent thermal samples drawn from the same disorder realization.

> The moral of the \(n\to0\) construction is that quenched disorder creates effective interactions between replicas.  Observables involving products of thermal averages become observables involving different replicas.  The analytic continuation to zero replicas removes the normalization factors that appeared in the original quenched averages.

## Learning problem \(N \to 1\)

We now turn to a different problem.  Instead of averaging over quenched random couplings, suppose we start from an ordinary statistical ensemble and then make noisy measurements of a configuration.  The measurement outcome is random, and after observing it we update our probability distribution for the underlying configuration.  This is a *Bayesian conditioning problem*.

The unmeasured ensemble has partition function
\[
Z = \int dS\, e^{-H[S]} .
\]
Here \(S\) denotes all microscopic degrees of freedom.  We imperfectly measure \(S_x\), or more generally a local observable \(O_x=f(S_x)\), with measurement outcome \(\{M_x\}_x\):
\[
M_x = S_x + \eta_x,
\qquad
\eta_x \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta^2).
\]
Equivalently, for a general measured observable \(O_x\), the conditional probability of the measurement outcome given the configuration (likelihood/ measurement model) is
\[
p(M|S)
=
\exp\!\left(
-\frac{1}{2\Delta^2}\sum_x (O_x - M_x)^2 + \ln d
\right),
\qquad
 d = \frac{1}{(2\pi\Delta^2)^{N_{\mathrm{meas}}/2}} .
\]
The parameter \(\Delta\) controls the measurement noise.  Small \(\Delta\) corresponds to a precise, strong measurement; large \(\Delta\) corresponds to a weak measurement.

Bayes' theorem gives the posterior distribution of configurations after the measurement outcome is known:

\[
\begin{aligned}
p(S|M)
&=
\frac{p(S)\,p(M|S)}{p(M)}
=
\frac{1}{Z[M]}\exp\!\left(-H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S,M]\right),
\end{aligned}
\]

with
\[
Z[M] = \int dS\, e^{-H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S,M]},
\qquad
p(M)=\frac{Z[M]}{Z},
\]
and
\[
H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S,M]
=
H[S]
+
\frac{1}{2\Delta^2}\sum_x (O_x - M_x)^2
-
\ln d .
\]
For a fixed measurement outcome \(M\), the posterior distribution \(p(S|M)\) is an ordinary normalized statistical ensemble.  However, we usually want to know what happens for a typical measurement outcome.  Therefore we must also average over \(M\), with \(M\) drawn from its physical distribution \(p(M)\).

A useful replicated observable is
\[
\mathbb{E}_M\!\left[(\langle S_x\rangle_M)^2\right]
=
\mathbb{E}_M\!\left[
\langle S_x^{(1)}\rangle_M \langle S_x^{(2)}\rangle_M
\right] .
\]
The two superscripts do not represent two different experiments.  They represent two independent posterior samples conditioned on the same measurement outcome.  This is directly analogous to the appearance of two replicas in \(q_{\mathrm{EA}}\), except that here the shared object is the measurement outcome rather than a quenched disorder realization.

### Replica trick for the posterior ensemble

Define
\[
p_K(S^1,S^2,\dots,S^K,M)
\]
to be the joint probability distribution of the measurement outcome together with \(K\) samples conditioned on that same outcome.  Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
p_K(S^1,S^2,\dots,S^K,M)
&=
p(M)\prod_{\alpha=1}^{K} p(S^\alpha|M)
\\
&=
\frac{Z[M]}{Z}\,\frac{1}{Z[M]^K}
\exp\!\left(
-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{K} H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S^\alpha,M]
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]

The factor \(Z[M]\) in \(p(M)\) is the key difference from the usual quenched-disorder problem.  It is the reason the replica limit will be \(N\to1\) rather than \(n\to0\).

Now use the replica trick in the form

\[
\begin{aligned}
p_K(S^1,S^2,\dots,S^K,M)
&=
\lim_{N\to 1}
\frac{Z[M]^{N-K}}{Z}
\exp\!\left(
-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{K} H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S^\alpha,M]
\right)
\\
&=
\lim_{N\to 1}
\frac{1}{Z}
\int dS^{K+1}\cdots dS^N\,
\exp\!\left(
-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S^\alpha,M]
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]

For integer \(N\ge K\), the additional replicas \(K+1,\dots,N\) simply represent the factor \(Z[M]^{N-K}\).  The continuation to \(N\to1\) then reproduces the correct weighting by \(p(M)\) and the correct posterior normalization.

Hence for any observable \(\bullet\) depending on \(K\) posterior samples, and possibly on \(M\),
\[
E^{(K)}[\bullet]
=
\frac{1}{Z}
\lim_{N\to 1}
\int dM\, dS^1\cdots dS^N\,
e^{-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S^\alpha,M]}\,
\bullet .
\]
This equation is the measurement analogue of the standard replica representation of disorder averages.  The measurement outcome \(M\) is common to all replicas, so integrating it out will generate interactions between replicas.

Define the replicated partition function
\[
Z_N
=
\int dM\, dS^1\cdots dS^N\,
e^{-\sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} H_{\mathrm{meas}}[S^\alpha,M]} .
\]
After integrating out \(M\), one obtains
\[
Z_N
=
\int dS^1\cdots dS^N\,
e^{-H_N[S^1,\dots,S^N]} ,
\]
with effective Hamiltonian
\[
H_N[S^1,\dots,S^N]
=
\sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} H[S^\alpha]
+
\frac{1}{2\Delta^2 N}
\sum_x\sum_{\alpha<\beta}
\bigl(O_x^{(\alpha)}-O_x^{(\beta)}\bigr)^2
-
(N-1)\ln d
+
\frac{N_{\mathrm{meas}}}{2}\ln N .
\]
The last two terms are additive constants.  
The interaction term has a simple interpretation.  Since all replicas are conditioned on the same measurement outcome, the posterior samples are encouraged to agree on the measured observable.  The term
\[
\frac{1}{2\Delta^2 N}
\sum_x\sum_{\alpha<\beta}
\bigl(O_x^{(\alpha)}-O_x^{(\beta)}\bigr)^2
\]
penalizes disagreement between replicas.  Stronger measurements, corresponding to smaller \(\Delta\), produce stronger locking between replicas.

Finally,
\[
\lim_{N\to 1} Z_N = Z,
\qquad
E^{(K)}[\bullet] = \lim_{N\to 1}\langle \bullet\rangle_N .
\]
Thus the \(N\to1\) theory converts averages over measurement outcomes into expectation values in a coupled replica theory.

> In the measurement problem, the replicas are not introduced to average over externally imposed disorder.  They are introduced because several posterior samples can share the same measurement outcome.  The shared measurement outcome generates a coupling that tends to lock replicas together in the measured channel.  The correct analytic continuation is \(N\to1\) because the probability of the measurement outcome contains one power of the posterior normalization, \(p(M)=Z[M]/Z\).

## Replica symmetry and the ordinary Nishimori line in the standard RBIM

**Setup**

Consider the random-bond Ising model

\[
H = -\sum_{\langle ij\rangle} J_{ij}\,\sigma_i\sigma_j,
\qquad J_{ij}=\pm 1,
\]

with bond distribution

\[
P(J_{ij}=+1)=1-p,\qquad P(J_{ij}=-1)=p,
\]

equivalently \(P(J)\propto e^{KJ}\) with \(K=\tfrac12\ln\!\left(\tfrac{1-p}{p}\right)\).
Replicate \(n\) times and disorder-average:

\[
\overline{Z^{\,n}}
=\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}
\overline{\exp\!\Big(\beta J_{ij}\sum_{a=1}^{n}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\Big)}.
\]

Per bond, with \(S\equiv\sum_{a=1}^{n}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\),

\[
\overline{e^{\beta J S}}
=(1-p)\,e^{\beta S}+p\,e^{-\beta S}.
\]

**Bond average on the Nishimori line:**

On the Nishimori line \(\beta=K\), using \(1-p=e^{K}/(2\cosh K)\) and \(p=e^{-K}/(2\cosh K)\),

\[
\overline{e^{\beta J S}}\Big|_{\beta=K}
=\frac{e^{K(S+1)}+e^{-K(S+1)}}{2\cosh K}
=\frac{\cosh\!\big(K(S+1)\big)}{\cosh K}.
\]

Therefore

\[
\overline{Z^{\,n}}\Big|_{\beta=K}
=(\cosh K)^{-N_b}\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}\cosh\!\Big(K\big(\textstyle\sum_a\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}+1\big)\Big),
\]

where \(N_b\) is the number of bonds. The crucial structural fact is that the bond
weight depends on \(S+1\) (not on \(S\) alone) and is an even function of its argument.

**Gauge change of variables:**

Choose arbitrary \(s_i\in\{\pm 1\}\) at each site, and define

\[
\widetilde{\sigma}_i^{(a)}\equiv s_i\,\sigma_i^{(a)}.
\]

Then \(S=s_i s_j \widetilde{S}\) with \(\widetilde{S}=\sum_a\widetilde{\sigma}_i^{(a)}\widetilde{\sigma}_j^{(a)}\), and the bond factor becomes

\[
\cosh\!\big(K(s_i s_j\widetilde{S}+1)\big)=\cosh\!\big(K(s_i s_j\widetilde{S}+(s_i s_j)^2)\big)=\cosh\!\big(Ks_i s_j(\widetilde{S}+s_i s_j)\big)=
\cosh\!\big(K(\widetilde{S}+s_i s_j)\big),
\]

where the second equality uses \((s_is_j)^2=1\) and the evenness of \(\cosh\):

The map \(\sigma\mapsto\widetilde\sigma=s\sigma\) is a bijection on \(\{\pm 1\}^{Nn}\), so
\(\sum_\sigma=\sum_{\widetilde\sigma}\). Renaming the dummy \(\widetilde\sigma\to\sigma\),

\[
\overline{Z^{\,n}}\Big|_{\beta=K}
=(\cosh K)^{-N_b}\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}\cosh\!\Big(K\big(\textstyle\sum_a\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}+s_is_j\big)\Big).
\]

Since the left-hand side is manifestly independent of \(\{s_i\}\), the right-hand
side is \(\{s_i\}\)-independent for *every* choice of \(\{s_i\}\).

### Promote \(s_i\) to an \((n{+}1)\)-th replica

Multiply the equation above by \(1=2^{-N}\sum_{\{s_i\}}1\) and identify \(s_i\equiv\sigma_i^{(n+1)}\).
Then \(s_is_j=\sigma_i^{(n+1)}\sigma_j^{(n+1)}\) and
\(\sum_a\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}+s_is_j=\sum_{a=1}^{n+1}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\equiv\widehat{S}_{ij}^{(n+1)}\).
Hence

\[
\boxed{\;
\overline{Z^{\,n}}\Big|_{\beta=K}
=\frac{(\cosh K)^{-N_b}}{2^{N}}
\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}_{a=1}^{n+1}}
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}\cosh\!\big(K\,\widehat{S}_{ij}^{(n+1)}\big).
\;}
\]

Equivalently, the effective \((n{+}1)\)-replica Hamiltonian is

\[
-\beta H_{n+1}
=\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\ln\cosh\!\Big(K\sum_{a=1}^{n+1}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\Big).
\]

**Enlarged symmetries and replica limits**

The \((n{+}1)\)-replica theory (equation above) has two manifest symmetries that
are absent (or hidden) in the original \(n\)-replica formulation:

#### (i) Permutation symmetry \(S_{n+1}\)
The action depends on the \(n{+}1\) replicas only through the symmetric sum
\(\sum_{a=1}^{n+1}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\), hence is invariant under
all \((n{+}1)!\) permutations of replica indices. The original theory had only
\(S_n\) permutation symmetry; the gauge averaging has enlarged it to \(S_{n+1}\).

#### (ii) \(\mathbb{Z}_2\) gauge invariance
For any choice \(\varepsilon_i\in\{\pm 1\}\),

\[
\sigma_i^{(a)}\longrightarrow\varepsilon_i\,\sigma_i^{(a)}\quad\forall\,a=1,\dots,n+1,
\]

sends \(\widehat{S}_{ij}^{(n+1)}\to\varepsilon_i\varepsilon_j\widehat{S}_{ij}^{(n+1)}\).
Since \(\cosh\) is even, the bond weight is invariant. The original
\(n\)-replica theory had only the *global* \(\mathbb{Z}_2\) flip; gauge
averaging promotes it to a *local* \(\mathbb{Z}_2\) at every site.

- The quenched-free-energy limit \(n\to 0\) of the original theory becomes
\(n+1\to 1\) in the enlarged formulation.

**Nishimori Ward Identity:**

Let,

\[
Q_{ij}^{(a)} := \sigma_i^{(a)} \sigma_j^{(a)}
\]

for the Ising case. The key point is that the extra replica \(a=n+1\) is the
replica-language image of the gauge/disorder degree of freedom. Therefore,
after the gauge rewrite,

\[
[\langle Q_{ij} \rangle]_J
\;\longleftrightarrow\;
\langle Q_{ij}^{(1)} s_{i}s_{j} \rangle
\;\longleftrightarrow\;
\big\langle Q_{ij}^{(1)} Q_{ij}^{(g)} \big\rangle ,
\;\longleftrightarrow\;
\big\langle Q_{ij}^{(1)} Q_{ij}^{(n+1)} \big\rangle ,
\]

where, 1 is the thermal copy and "\(g\)" is the copy coming from gauge; whereas

\[
[\langle Q_{ij} \rangle_J^2]
\;\longleftrightarrow\;
\big\langle Q_{ij}^{(1)} Q_{ij}^{(2)} \big\rangle .
\]

Now use the enlarged permutation symmetry. Since replicas \(2\) and \(n+1\) are
interchangeable in the \(S_{n+1}\)-invariant theory,

\[
\big\langle Q_{ij}^{(1)} Q_{ij}^{(n+1)} \big\rangle
=
\big\langle Q_{ij}^{(1)} Q_{ij}^{(2)} \big\rangle .
\]

Translating back to disorder-averaged observables gives the Nishimori Ward
identity

\[
[\langle Q_{ij} \rangle]_J
=
[\langle Q_{ij} \rangle_J^2].
\]

More generally, the same argument gives

\[
\boxed{
[\langle Q_{ij} \rangle_J^{\,2m-1}]
=
[\langle Q_{ij} \rangle_J^{\,2m}],
\qquad m=1,2,\dots}
\]

because one may replace the special replica \((n+1)\) by an ordinary thermal
replica using \(S_{n+1}\) symmetry.

## Replica symmetry and the higher Nishimori line in the Bayesian learning problem

**Replica theory for Gaussian bond measurements**

We now specialize the general \(N\to 1\) construction to the
two-dimensional Ising model, with unmeasured distribution
\[
p_0(\sigma)=\frac{1}{Z}\exp\!\left(\beta\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i\sigma_j\right),
\qquad
\tau_{\langle ij\rangle}(\sigma)\equiv \sigma_i\sigma_j .
\]
Suppose that on each bond we make a Gaussian measurement of
\(\tau_{\langle ij\rangle}\) and obtain a real outcome
\(M_{\langle ij\rangle}\in\mathbb{R}\) with likelihood
\[
p(\{M_{\langle ij\rangle}\}|\sigma)
\propto
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}
\exp\!\left[
-\frac{\Delta}{2}\bigl(M_{\langle ij\rangle}-\sigma_i\sigma_j\bigr)^2
\right],
\]
For fixed measurement record \(\{M_{\langle ij\rangle}\}\), the posterior ensemble is
\[
p(\sigma|\{M_{\langle ij\rangle}\})
=
\frac{1}{Z[\{M_{\langle ij\rangle}\}]}
\exp\!\left[
\beta\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i\sigma_j
-\frac{\Delta}{2}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\bigl(M_{\langle ij\rangle}-\sigma_i\sigma_j\bigr)^2
\right].
\]

To average observables over the physical distribution of measurement
outcomes, we introduce \(N\) posterior samples sharing the same
measurement record and then analytically continue \(N\to 1\). The
replicated partition function is
\[
Z_N^{\mathrm{G}}
=
\int \prod_{\langle ij\rangle} dM_{\langle ij\rangle}
\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}
\exp\!\left[
\beta\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
-\frac{\Delta}{2}\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\bigl(M_{\langle ij\rangle}-\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\bigr)^2
\right].
\]
The Gaussian integral over each \(M_{\langle ij\rangle}\) can be done exactly.
Up to an additive constant, one finds
\[
Z_N^{\mathrm{G}}
\propto
\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}
\exp\!\left[
\beta\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+\frac{\Delta}{2N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sum_{a\neq b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
\right].
\]
Equivalently, the exact replicated Hamiltonian is
\[
\boxed{
H_N^{\mathrm{G}}
=
-\beta\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
-\frac{\Delta}{2N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sum_{a\neq b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
+\text{const}.}
\]
Thus Gaussian bond measurements lead exactly to a pairwise-coupled
replica theory, and the physical measurement problem is obtained by the
analytic continuation \(N\to 1\), the
Gaussian higher Nishimori line is therefore
\[
\beta=\Delta.
\]

**Binary measurements.**
If instead the bond measurement outcome is discrete,
\(m_{\langle ij\rangle}=\pm 1\), a convenient likelihood is
\[
p(\{m_{\langle ij\rangle}\}|\sigma)
=
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}
\frac{e^{\tilde\gamma\, m_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i\sigma_j}}
{2\cosh \tilde\gamma}
=
\prod_{\langle ij\rangle}
\frac{1+\gamma\, m_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i\sigma_j}{2},
\qquad
\gamma=\tanh \tilde\gamma .
\]
After replication and summing over the binary outcomes,
\[
Z_N^{\mathrm{bin}}
\propto
\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}
\exp\!\left[
\beta\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\log\cosh\!\left(
\tilde\gamma\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
\right)
\right].
\]
For weak measurements,
\[
\log\cosh x = \frac{x^2}{2}-\frac{x^4}{12}+O(x^6),
\]
so
\[
\boxed{
Z_N^{\mathrm{bin}}
\propto
\sum_{\{\sigma^{(a)}\}}
\exp\!\left[
\beta\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+\frac{\tilde\gamma^2}{2}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left(\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\right)^2
-\frac{\tilde\gamma^4}{12}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left(\sum_{a=1}^{N}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\right)^4
+\cdots
\right].}
\]
> The quadratic term gives the same pairwise replica coupling as the
> Gaussian theory at the physical continuation point \(N\to 1\) after the
> identification \(\Delta \sim \tilde\gamma^2\). However, unlike the
> Gaussian case, binary measurements also generate irreducible
> four-replica, six-replica, and higher terms. Therefore the simple
> pairwise replica Hamiltonian is exact for Gaussian measurements, while
> for binary measurements it should be viewed as a long-distance
> effective theory. Nevertheless, one expects the same universal \(N\to 1\)
> critical behavior at the tricritical point, even though the exact
> short-distance identities of the Gaussian problem are no longer present.

**Higher order Nishimori line and gauge symmetry:**

Start from
\[
H_N^{\mathrm G}
=
-\beta \sum_{a=1}^N \sum_{\langle ij\rangle} \sigma_i^{(a)} \sigma_j^{(a)}
-\frac{\Delta}{2N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}\sum_{a\neq b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
+\text{const.}
\]
On the Nishimori line,
\[
\beta=\Delta,
\]
so
\[
-H_N^{\mathrm G}
=
\beta \sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left[
\sum_{a=1}^N \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+\frac{1}{2N}\sum_{a\neq b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
\right]
+\text{const.}
\]
Using
\[
\left(\sum_{a=1}^N \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\right)^2
=
N+\sum_{a\neq b}\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)},
\]
we can complete the square bond by bond:
\[
\sum_{a=1}^N \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
+\frac{1}{2N}\sum_{a\neq b}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}\sigma_i^{(b)}\sigma_j^{(b)}
=
\frac{1}{2N}
\left(
\sum_{a=1}^N \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)} + N
\right)^2
+\text{const.}
\]
Hence,
\[
-H_N^{\mathrm G}
=
\frac{\beta}{2N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left(
\sum_{a=1}^N \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)} + N
\right)^2
+\text{const.}
\]
with,
\[
1=\frac{1}{2^{N_s}}\sum_{\{s_i=\pm1\}}1
\]
and make the change of variables
\[
\sigma_i^{(a)} \to s_i \sigma_i^{(a)} \qquad (a=1,\dots,N).
\]
Then
\[
-H_N^{\mathrm G}
=
\frac{\beta}{2N}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left(
\sum_{a=1}^N \sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)} + N\, s_i s_j
\right)^2
+\text{const.}
\]
Thus the Nishimori-line theory has an emergent local \(\mathbb{Z}_2\)
redundancy. In the physical replica limit \(N\to 1\), this becomes
\[
-H_N^{\mathrm G}
=
\frac{\beta}{2}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left(\sum_{a=1}^{N}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)} + s_i s_j
\right)^2
+\text{const.}
\]
If we identify
\[
s_i \equiv \sigma_i^{(N+1)},
\]
we obtain
\[
-H_N^{\mathrm G}
=
\frac{\beta}{2}\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}
\left(\sum_{a=1}^{N+1}
\sigma_i^{(a)}\sigma_j^{(a)}
\right)^2
+\text{const.}
\]
So on the Gaussian Nishimori line the \(N\to 1\) theory acquires one
additional replica, and in this form the local gauge structure is
manifest:
\[
\sigma_i^{(a)} \to \eta_i \sigma_i^{(a)}, \eta_i=\pm1.
\]

The total symmetry thus becomes

\[
\mathcal{G}= (\mathbb{Z}_2)^{N_s} \times S_{N+1}
\]

> In other words, on the Gaussian Nishimori line the measurement problem reorganizes into an integer \((N+1)\)-replica theory with enlarged symmetry
> \[
> (\mathbb{Z}_2)^{N_s} \times S_{N+1}.
> \]
> In the physical limit \(N \to 1\), this becomes a genuine two-replica gauge-invariant theory, so the extra replica is precisely the replica-language image of the Nishimori gauge degree of freedom.
